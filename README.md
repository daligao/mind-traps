## 🎮 What is Mind Traps?

Mind Traps is an interactive psychology game that teaches you **40 cognitive biases and psychology laws** through real-world scenarios.

### Features

✅ **320+ Scenarios** — 8 different scenarios for each of 40 laws  
✅ **Instant Feedback** — Learn why your answer was right or wrong  
✅ **No Login** — Play completely anonymously  
✅ **Free Forever** — MIT open-source license  
✅ **Mobile Ready** — Works on desktop, tablet, and phone  
✅ **Progress Tracking** — See which laws trip you up most

## 🧠 The 40 Psychology Laws

### Category 1: Cognitive Biases (10 laws)
- Confirmation Bias — seeking confirming information
- Availability Heuristic — judging by easily recalled examples
- Dunning-Kruger Effect — incompetent overestimate competence
- Halo Effect — one trait influences overall judgment
- Anchoring Bias — first number sticks to perception
- Recency Bias — overweighting recent information
- Negativity Bias — focusing on negative information
- Sunk Cost Fallacy — past investment influences future choices
- Survivorship Bias — only seeing winners
- Status Quo Bias — preferring things to stay same

### Category 2: Decision-Making (10 laws)
- Hyperbolic Discounting — valuing immediate over future rewards
- Loss Aversion — fear of loss > hope of gain
- Scarcity Bias — limited options feel more valuable
- Planning Fallacy — underestimating time needed
- Choice Overload — too many options paralyze decisions
- Contrast Effect — misjudging value by recent comparisons
- FOMO — fear of missing out drives decisions
- Groupthink — group pressure overrides critical thinking
- Herding Behavior — following crowd without analysis
- Illusion of Control — believing we control random events

### Category 3: Social & Behavioral (10 laws)
- Conformity Bias — adapting to group norms
- Authority Bias — over-trusting authority
- Likability Bias — attractive people seem smarter
- Moral Licensing — feeling virtuous after one good act
- Dunning-Kruger Inverse — experts underestimate knowledge
- Backfire Effect — evidence strengthens opposite belief
- Foot-in-the-Door — small commitments lead to larger
- Door-in-the-Face — large request followed by smaller
- Reciprocity Bias — tendency to return favors
- Curse of Knowledge — difficulty understanding others don't know

### Category 4: Logic & Reasoning (10 laws)
- Correlation ≠ Causation — confusing relationship with cause
- Hasty Generalization — conclusions from limited data
- False Consensus — overestimating agreement
- Fundamental Attribution Error — blaming personality not situation
- Actor-Observer Bias — different explanations for self vs others
- Hindsight Bias — "I knew it all along"
- Gambler's Fallacy — past results affect future probability
- Base Rate Fallacy — ignoring statistical likelihood
- Conjunction Fallacy — specific scenario seems more likely
- Sunk Cost Tradeoff — weighing efforts by past work

## 📊 How It Works

1. **Read a Scenario** — Get a real-world decision situation
2. **Choose Your Answer** — Pick which psychology law is at play
3. **Get Instant Feedback** — Learn why that answer was right/wrong
4. **See the Explanation** — Understand the psychology behind it
5. **Track Progress** — See your score for each law category

### Example Scenario

> **Scenario:** A startup founder invested $200,000 over 2 years. The business is clearly failing, but she invests another $50,000 instead of shutting down.  
> **Question:** What psychology law explains this?
> 
> A) Sunk Cost Fallacy  
> B) Survivorship Bias  
> C) Loss Aversion  
> D) Status Quo Bias

**Correct Answer:** A) Sunk Cost Fallacy  
**Explanation:** She's using past investment ($200k) as justification for future investment, even though the $200k is gone either way.
